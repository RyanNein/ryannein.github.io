The Phenomenal Field as the World-Context for Abstract Objects as Intentional Entities

Philosophical entities of any kind exist within a context called a world. For most conventional philosophers, the world is something familiar to common sense. Common sense, as cultural lineage has passed it down to us, tells of a context outside the individual egos of philosophers that is comprised of a postulate called matter. This context is what entities or objects inhabit, as they take up the space and time of the world and are subject to physical constraints.

The world of the physicalist or materialist is not a given though, and it serves doctrinal purposes for defining the bounds of acceptable philosophy. All philosophy requires axiomatic assertions for the sake of foundation, and the world is one necessary foundation. The world, whatever that may be, can be said to dissolve into mystery at the most basic level. From a physical perspective, a body dissolves into atoms, then into particles, then into quarks, and so on until some bedrock of brute facts is eventually discovered. From a biological perspective, the human body the self relies upon dissolves into cells inside of microbiomes that have selves distinct from the human host, but upon which the host depends without a conscious means of interfacing. Philosophy is no different from these disciplines in that it seeks answers to questions that are so grand in their mysteriousness, yet so mundane in their average everydayness.

The world can mean many different things, and sometimes unlikely candidates for worldhood are valuable for human understanding. For Wittgenstein, the world is the totality of facts, rather than of things. A naïve interpretation of Wittgenstein’s atoms and objects would be to think he is doing simple mereology, but that interpretation ignores the worldly context of his project. The world does not have to be made of matter, it does not have to be rooted in the notion of physicality at all. Propositions have structure, and because of that they can serve as the basis for a world. For Heidegger, the world is known through being-with and being-in. Although less formal than Wittgenstein’s world of facts, Heidegger’s world is one built up of significances. The world is embodied as if part of the self, rather than being an alien material body that collides with a self’s human body.

Whatever the world is, it is not a physical object amongst physical objects. The world is a context, and as such must be something other than what is conditioned by that context. If the physical world was of the same nature as physical objects, it would be subject to the same categorical rules and embodied constraints as its objects and would no longer serve as a world. If ontology is to be truly flat, it would be better to remove the world than to explain it, for otherwise it serves no purpose and is not even a world at all. It is possible that there is no worldly context for entities, though the nature of the mind seems to necessitate, or at least desire, one for the sake of rational thought.

One conception of the world is that of a substrate. Physical objects necessitate space and time to exist, and in this way use the makeup of the world-context as a medium for their own existence. But if simplicity is to be desired, then a substrate that provides context is undesirable because it implies a bedrock of brute existence within the lowest degree of substrate, and introduces hierarchy that would require ontological justification. The world cannot be the same as its entities nor different from them.

The world is not a being among other beings, but the world does seem to need Being so as to be anything. Entities in the world all have Being, and without Being within their world, the world would be invisible. Being is what is common to all entities, not because of some metaphysical essence, but because sensibility requires a form for signification.

The entities of the world, which have Being, are often called “objects,” and the world is typically thought to be comprised of objects. It is intuitive to Western philosophers, who have absorbed the Greek tradition, to imagine the world as having a “substance,” and substance is to be found in objects. Substance is a confused concept with baggage rooted in Aristotle’s lack of distinction between ontology and language. In some analyses, substance is the essence of objects that provides a ground for properties, but in other analyses any predicate is found in substances, which leads to speculations about abstract objects. This interpretation of substance is a meme within Western thought, whereas Buddhist philosophy, for example, has built its metaphysics around emptiness, or lack of substantial existence. Even modern analytic philosophers like Bertrand Russell have abandoned the concept of substance when reflecting critically on the lack of transparency in language.

No matter what entities one fills their concept of the world with, one will find objects there. If the world is made out of facts, those facts are objects. If the world is made out of functions, those functions are objects. Objecthood takes over anything with being as the sole means of expressing the existence of thing-ness. Object oriented ontology has even capitalized on this all-encompassing nature of the term “object” to claim that even relations are actually objects. The pervasiveness of objects is also found in computer science, which has a digital ontology that parallels and deviates from philosophical ontology. In object oriented programming, everything is an object from data to variables to keywords. This may or may not reflect the ontology of the world containing the computer, but is nonetheless an epistemic tool for compartmentalizing and organizing complex data into forms that are intuitive for human cognition to comprehend and operate within. There is something valuable about the all-encompassing object for these and other reasons, but the situation of objecthood needs to be investigated so as to understand confusions and avoid equivocation.

Signs, or linguistic units, typically have referents, but the term “object” lacks a referent or any kind of sign-vehicle. If someone intends upon a cup, we assume that a physical cup is the referent of that intentional act. But the physical cup is only epistemically accessible through more intention, which creates a secondary intention for the physical cup that the original intention refers to. In no way can a physical object in itself enter into the linguistic world-context. Object is not a particular, it is a universal, and potentially the most universal term available depending on one’s metaphysical frame. The universal term “object” is an abstract idea and is not a general idea (to use Berkeley’s language) capable of representation through a sign-vehicle. Objecthood is considered so universal that to the uncritical mind it encompasses all of what is designated as reality. “Object,” despite not carrying reference or sign-vehiclehood, does have associative properties that give insight into the biases of philosophers. Physical objects are classified as discrete, solid, and unitary, and these properties are carried along with the term “object” when it is extended beyond the physical context. What occurs during philosophical confusion is that philosophers go beyond physicality while retaining traces of associative object properties. This results in new and radically diverse linguistic entities that all share in a common signifier.

We see a pattern in how objecthood is expanded in scope within what appears as a likely and natural process to Western philosophers. But this likely progression of objecthood from physical things “out there” to non-physical things is genetically epistemological, not truly a depiction of how objecthood functions independently of diachronic progression. The mind interacts with representations; we typically say they are representations of objects. This model is such that objects are something physical and representations are something other than physical, but dependent upon the physical. When objecthood has progressed beyond the physical it reaches representations and they themselves become objects. Thus, we have objects that are representations of objects with no foundation outside of the universal cross-categorical unit.

The Western genealogy of objects is a progression that actually reverses the underlying model of acquisition. What a human interacts with and has access to are representations of intention, and representations are the entirety of the sensible world-context. Beyond high speculation about abstract objects, even plain physical objects are ultimately the product of speculation as they are posited to exist beyond phenomenal appearances. Representations are the given of the world, whereas physical objects are merely guessed at. When we ask what the hardness of a rock is, we find that it is a representation. When we try to describe the color of an object-in-itself we are unable to do so because color is only a representation. The bias of Western philosophy is to treat the postulate as the starting point for knowledge and to build up philosophy on a shaky foundation of speculation, resulting in every possible entity being modeled upon unknowable object units. In the backwards processing of conventional philosophy, eventually objecthood extends to the invisible, acausal, non-spatiotemporal: abstract objects.

George Berkely attempted to deny primary qualities and collapsed all objects into ideas. This is a more honest approach to entity units because it works off of accessible entities, but he failed to properly separate ideas from noumenal referents, resulting in idealism. If an idea of a tree is in a mind, this does not entail that a noumenal referent of such an intention is also in the mind. The noumenon is inaccessible in language, and as such the quantification of world units can only apply to the sensible, which excludes noumenal and physical objects.

Metaphysics begins with the given of representation and should construct arguments rooted in an understanding of epistemic limits. Instead of the physical object, intentionality is the basis for constructing a unit and category of existence. Sensibility is the condition for knowledge and anything not directly sensible is speculative in nature. Sensibility is common to phenomenal appearances intended upon, and this is the best foundation for erecting any kind of metaphysics. The context that provides the world in which representations appear is called the phenomenal field; the phenomenal field is the world. What appears on the phenomenal field and is intended upon will be called entities rather than objects to avoid falling into the associative trap of thinking the world’s content is modeled on physical things.

Abstract objects cause confusion because they are modeled on the traditional physical notion of objects. Abstract objects are not hard to comprehend when viewed simply as phenomenal, but when this happens, phenomenal is misconstrued as psychological. Psychologism makes the assumption that phenomena are the product of mentation and or are rooted in brain functions. The phenomenal field is not a mind in the strict sense, it is the stage of appearances revealed by intention. The mind is not knowable through the phenomenal field and is a speculative thing no different from a physical object. Like how a noumenon is an otherworldly thing-in-itself beyond sensibility, mind is something postulated to exist in-itself behind phenomenal appearances rather than beyond them. The common assumption that physical objects are the origin of representations is shared by the assumption that a mind is also an origin of appearances, just with a different role to play in an acquisition process.

The phenomenal field is the totality of sense data. This is, in a loose sense, a “mind,” but it is not the classical mind. The conventional notion of the world is split into subjects and objects, and in this view the phenomenal field would in its entirety be considered a subject. But within the phenomenal field there is another partition, that between mental representations and physical representations. An image of a rock is a physical representation, but the “mind’s eye” is also capable of representations divorced from physicality. Linguistic thoughts as intentional entities are of this latter kind, for example. Therefore, within the “mind” that is the phenomenal field, there lies more mind and more unmind.

The point of the phenomenal partitioning is to demonstrate how subjects and objects exist of each other. Plato’s realm of ideas (his instantiation of abstract objects) is a mirror of the subject; how else could ideas exist independent of people? Therefore, like how the world splits into subjects and objects, and how the phenomenal field splits into mind and unmind, Plato split his world into the realm of nature (object) and the realm of ideas (subject). Association is the basis for categorical distinction, and Platonism fails to treat potentially abstract entities as anything other than mirrors of other more immediate categories of entities.

Because the world is the phenomenal field, abstract objects beyond the field are entirely speculative. It is already a matter of speculation to believe in rocks, let alone abstract objects. The problem is that abstract objects are thought to be something behind appearances and they do not match the postulated associative features of the two predominant categories of being, physical and mental, held by Western philosophers. But when abstract objects are treated as and understood to be phenomenal in nature, there are no unusual or problematic features in need of explanation. Abstract objects have the properties of ideas, after all Plato referred to the abstract objects as both forms and ideas. Abstract objects are non-physical by definition and are invisible to sensibility when treated as things-in-themselves. But there is nothing unique to abstract objects apart from the basic and common features of intentional entities. The number 3, for example, can be intended upon no differently than any other sensible entity. Abstract objects are only invisible when treated as speculative objects and they are entirely visible to sensibility as phenomena. The only thing that separates abstract objects from ideas is that ideas are associated with psychology, but ideas are phenomenal appearances completely divorced from the mind-in-itself.

A better explanation than speculation for abstract objects is intentionality, which states that phenomena are revealed through the phenomenal field, in which they are “intended” upon to find their aboutness. An intentional entity does not require physicality or even independent existence beyond the phenomenal field. Anything and everything within the boundaries of the possibilities of phenomena can be intentional entities.

Because the world is made of intentional entities, there is a limit to what can be spoken about. When one attempts to discover the referent of an intentional sign, the referent is always another intentional entity. The intentional basis of world units has implications for what is sensible and what is nonsensical in philosophical discourse. Every intentional entity has Being, because without Being such an entity would not be sensible to the phenomenal field. This also entails that everything that can potentially enter into discourse already has Being before any linguistic instantiation. Before the computer was invented, the possibility of a computer was already possible within the categorical structure of the world-context, so a computer has always had Being as long as the world was shaped by the same stable categories as it now is. Everything possible has Being, and anything without Being is nonsensical or mute. Abstract objects are no different from any other intentional entity in that they are known through intention conditioned by the possibility of Being. All abstract objects, whether they be numbers or properties or universals, have Being and are known through their Being.

Because all entities are predicated by Being, everything exists. But existence has multiple meanings and philosophers often equivocate on what it means to exist. Kant claimed that existence was not a predicate in opposition to the sleight of hand endorsed by the ontological argument. Being is in fact a predicate in that it operates no differently than other predicates, but because everything sensible is predicated with Being, it lacks meaning outside of establishing the boundaries of sensible discourse. There are multiple kinds of being, and not every being is the universal predicate Being with a capital B. Existential-being is another predicate in the ontico-epistemological context of predication. Things predicated with existential-being are thought to exist in some way more concrete than simple Being. For example, a cup in front of me has both existential-being and Being, which demarcates its ontological status from a fictional character, which only has Being and not existential-being. When philosophers argue over the existence of abstract objects, they often conflate these two forms of being and make the mistake of assuming that because an abstract object has Being, it therefore exists. But Being only guarantees sensibility within the phenomenal field, not existential instantiation. What matters more is whether or not abstract objects have existential-being, but it must be understood that even existential-being is merely a predicate and nothing more.

To formalize the nature of Being as a predicate, it can be said that everything that can be quantified has Being (B): ∀x B(x). Because existential-being is a sub-predicate of Being, everything with existential-being (E) also has Being: ∀x (E(x) → B(x)). Because existential-being is a predicate, it only denotes that an entity exists, without any ontological commitment outside of intension. Language is categorically determined by logic, and logic has nothing to say about the ontology of a noumenal or physical world. When we say that a physical cup has existential-being, we are only using the logical form of predication to assign it properties in a field of predicative properties, none of which grant any kind of ontological status. The phenomenal field is the world, and nothing in language or logic can extend itself beyond the boundaries of the world.

The use of intentional entities in predication lacks any kind of ontological commitment outside of the basic feature of Being in all entities. Quine’s idea of ontological commitment states that when things are quantified over, such as with the existential quantifier, they are granted an ontological status of existing, such that ontology is a mirror of what is quantified. This theory has several problems. The theory of ontological commitment does not distinguish between types of being. Being is a condition of quantification in the first place because everything quantifiable already has Being by being sensible. The domain of quantifiability is the entirety of sensible intentions, so instantiating quantifier logic with entities says nothing about the ontology of those entities. Committing to Being is trivial and reveals nothing about the world because everything that could potentially be quantified has Being as a condition for entering into logical discourse at all. If one states that three is prime, it could be formalized as, ∃x (Three(x) ∧ Prime(x) ∧ B(x)). This statement makes no intensionally existential claims because existential-being is not invoked and neither three nor prime is predicated by existential-being. The only thing such a statement is committed to is that three and prime have Being, but this is trivially true because they are both entities of intention. It could also be stated that, ∀x (Prime(x) → B(x)), in which case nothing prime has existential-being and there is no confusion about the status of numbers. Logic is a tool for organizing intentional entities within the phenomenal field and says nothing about an external world. The theory of ontological commitment assumes that language is a transparent mirror of the external world, which oversteps the boundaries of logic and is ultimately a category mistake.

It is common in Western philosophy to believe that the world is structured by categories, and this is not wrong, however, the model used for understanding categories is bloated and rooted in classic confusion over noumenality reaching into linguistic discourse. The color red is a qualia, and one might say that red is shaped by qualia or that qualia is the condition that gives red its form. But any metaphysic that follows this pattern implicitly relies upon transcendence. For example, there is endless debate over whether or not God could create a world that is illogical. The assumption here is that the categories are transcendent such that God relied upon logic for creating a logical world. But transcendent categories are entirely speculative and violate the strictly immanent domain of intentional discourse. Such debate assumes a noumenal world-context that is unjustified. Categories are intentional entities and predicates with Being no different than any other unit of discourse. It is more accurate to say that red is predicated by qualia than to say qualia at all composes or conditions red. There is nothing beyond the phenomenal field that can enter into sensible discourse.

Form and content are equiprimordial associative clusters that classify categorical entities. Categories appear on the phenomenal field no differently than other intentional entities and operate as predicates for organizing the same phenomena that they are. Categories are content in the web of predication, and they generate form through their organization amongst entities.

There is no need to postulate a means by which reality is structured because reality is already fundamentally abstract. A newborn can let a table enter into their phenomenal field without any means of classifying it and without knowing what a table is. In the same way, reality discloses itself to one’s phenomenal field, and it becomes something recognizable by applying predicative categories to it. When viewing an abstract table, one can assign it predicates such as “solid,” and “on-the-floor,” that give the intention structure, but that structure is not intrinsic to the physical or noumenal table, it is applied to the phenomenon through categorical predication. The intentional entity that is the table is solid only because it is predicated by solidity.

When an amateur artist attempts to draw a still-life, they resort to drawing icons and symbols of what they are trying to depict. Amateurs draw circles with dots for eyes and triangles for noses, despite the source material appearing differently. A professional artist suspends all categories of classification to absorb the raw sense data, and they translate that raw data into their representation. This pattern demonstrates how it is the untrained mind that necessitates categories for interpretation. In the acquisition process, it is the unstructured data that appears first and is the basis of reality within the phenomenal field. The phenomenal field requires no structure independent of classification because all structure is imposed upon the phenomenal field by an agent. Metaphysics should parallel the process of developing artistic skill by removing structural training wheels from the foundation of thought.

The debate over abstract objects is rooted in a misunderstanding of categories that treats them as transcendent. The traditional Western metaphysics is such that there is a noumenal world structured by transcendent categories, and this model allows for any kind of speculation surrounding “realms” that share in a categorical structure. Philosophers assume that because abstract objects appear to be subject to categories, that they must be in some sense “real” and that reality must take the form of existential instantiation. But when categories are recognized as immanent intentional entities on the phenomenal field, all metaphysical realms dissolve. Abstract objects are themselves categorical because they share in the basic constituents of the world-context by being intentional entities. Abstract objects do have Being, and in this sense are real, but the reality of abstract objects is a trivial fact brought about by their very presence in discourse. A more well-formed question would be whether or not abstract objects have existential-being, but even this question could only result in epistemological models that do not grant ontological status.

The question of abstract objects does not elucidate anything about reality and is the tired residue of classic Western biases that have caused much of the history of philosophy to be confused and fruitless. Being is not a monolithic category of reality, it divides in types of existence that can only predicate intentional entities. Discourse has a domain exclusively comprised of intentional entities, and such entities need not resemble objects derived from an inverted metaphysics of noumenality. The model of the world that is most honest to humanity’s epistemic limits is the phenomenal field, which is an unstructured stage of appearances that can be organized by the predicative units that make it up. There is no possible discourse in which the existence of abstract objects in-themselves could be established, and any attempt to do so relied upon flawed models of the world and speculative assumptions disguised as unquestioned premises.
